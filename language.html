Predictive vs. Causal Language

This section introduces how to interpret interpret the results of statistical procedures and to effectively communicate this understanding to others.

We seriously reccomend the initial thinkCausal modules and Individual, Group and Population Effects module before covering this one, as the content is most helpful after solidifying that knowledge.

So Far, (not) So Good
The challenge in accurately conveying statistical results is amplified when the potential exists for results to be interpreted causally. As a result, various communication approaches have evolved over the years to guard against unwarranted causal interpretations.

Arguably, the most common strategy is to use the word “association” in situations where there is inadequate evidence for causal intent. These wording choices, however, may be insufficient to prevent causal intent by the reader.

One reason for this is that there may be other linguistic signals in such a description that would still imply a causal connection. Another is that, even with careful wording, there is a strong proclivity in human reasoning towards causal attribution.

Background
Why is it important for students to be able to distinguish between study results that provide evidence for a causal relationship versus those that are merely associational? The primary reason is that causal relationships can provide guidance for decision making in ways that associational evidence does not.

For instance, if we know that there is an association between using an educational tool and subsequent test scores, it might be interesting to try to explain why that association is present. Are more prepared or confident students more likely to adopt the tool? Or is the tool adding value on its own?

If instead we knew the relationship were causal, then we might be moved to adopt this tool in the classroom. If we adopted the tool for more general use based on merely a positive association and the causal effect was in fact zero or negative, then this would be a waste of time and money and other scarce resources (e.g., teacher goodwill) in the educational system.

Using 'Associated with'
Our example finding might be summarized as a negative association between tool exposure and test score and be described in the following way: “The online tool was associated with a 20-point decrease in test scores.”

What is wrong with this wording? It is a difference in the precise meanings of 'association' between statisticians and other fields. Also, the meaning we intend to have is often not sufficiently well understood by other audiences. Often 'association' has colloquial meanings that lean closer to causality, like in institutional or semi-formal language where 'association' is neutral but strongly implies involvement or responsibility.

"He was found with associated individuals."
"Items associated with the burglary were recovered."
"They're associated with that crowd."

Moreover, this interpretation also includes the word "decrease," and thus seems to imply that the tool use decreased test scores for students (on average) by 20 points. A logical implication of such a causal interpretation would be that if the students who used the reading tool had instead not used the tool, they would have scored an average of 20 points higher than what we observed at the end of the year. Such an interpretation, however, is not merited.

Psychological Barriers
Psychologically, people are predisposed to these types of misinterpretations of the evidence due to the human tendency to make meaning of experiences through causal interpretations.

Related work has demonstrated that people explain and predict behavior similarly to how they understand and tell stories; this requires understanding the sequence of events and making inferences about the mechanism behind each event.

Depending on the magnitude of the impact of prior beliefs, it seems possible that even if non-causal study results are communicated clearly, the audience may still be inclined to interpret them causally if the causal conclusions are consistent with their prior beliefs.

Reccomended Interpretations
Using a college student anxiety and vaping example,

Explicitly Not Causal:
Researchers found that college students who vaped regularly had higher levels of anxiety compared to those who didn’t vape. Skeptics argue that this difference in outcomes could be explained by the fact that students with higher levels of anxiety are more likely to decide to vape.

Researchers found that college students who vaped regularly had higher levels of anxiety compared to those who didn't vape. It's possible that students who decided to vape had higher levels of anxiety when they made the decision to start vaping.

Explicitly Causal:
Researchers have found that vaping caused an increase in anxiety levels among college students.

Researchers have found that vaping led to an increase in anxiety levels among college students.
